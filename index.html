01
Strategic Context · The Problem

The coordination system was a person. That's a scale ceiling, not a process.

SoulSpiti connects customers to a regional network of independent craft suppliers. In early 2024, the entire order fulfilment operation — supplier assignment, production tracking, follow-ups, exception handling — ran through a single person's WhatsApp thread and mental model. This is a common pattern in early-stage operations: founder knowledge substitutes for system design.

The strategic problem isn't that things break. It's that the failure mode of a human-dependent system is invisible until it's catastrophic. At 100 orders/month it was survivable friction. At 150+ it was a growth blocker. A high-demand date triggered the moment of visibility: six customers received nothing, cancellation rate hit 30%, and complaints became public. The event wasn't the problem — it was the diagnostic.

The real constraint — not a supplier problem
The incident was blamed on a supplier misreading a message. But the underlying structure was: no order format, no acknowledgement loop, no capacity visibility, no fallback routing, no audit trail. Every element of a coordination system was missing. Fixing supplier behaviour wouldn't have changed the outcome — the same failure would recur in a different form. The intervention had to be structural.

02
Diagnosis · Reading the Signal

The first design was right in structure. Wrong about the users it was designed for.

The initial solution was logically sound: automate dispatch via WhatsApp, structure the order format, log acknowledgements. It moved fast. It went live quickly. And it surfaced a 68% supplier non-response rate within days.

That number was not a failure — it was diagnostic information. The structure was right; the model of the user was wrong. Craft supplier networks have specific, non-obvious patterns of technology use that don't surface from a product brief. They require observation.

What the data pointed to
Three systematic failure patterns in the response data: timing (messages arriving outside the windows suppliers actually check their phones), format (reply parsing too rigid for how this user group communicates), and information hierarchy (message structure placing job details before the one piece of information suppliers evaluate first — payment terms). None of these are generic automation mistakes. All three are industry-specific. Field research was the only path to them.

"The first version didn't fail because it was poorly built. It failed because craft supplier networks have specific patterns of technology use that you can only learn by being there."

03
User Research · Behavioural Findings

Designing for how suppliers actually work, not how we assumed they would.

I visited three suppliers at their workshops — 6+ hours each, observing rather than interviewing. The goal was to answer four questions: when do they engage with their phones, how do they evaluate incoming work, what do they read first, and what do they skip entirely?

⏰
Phone engagement happens in 3 narrow windows
Suppliers are craftspeople first. Phones are checked during work breaks — morning, midday, evening. Messages outside these windows go unseen for hours regardless of urgency.
→ Redesigned: send scheduling logic
💰
Payment is the first decision filter, always
Before reading any job detail, suppliers scan for the payment figure. Buried payment = slower decision. Payment in line 1 = faster acknowledgement. This is a cognitive load pattern, not a preference.
→ Redesigned: message hierarchy
✂️
Two lines is the real reading limit
Longer messages were handed off or skipped. This is not about literacy — it's about attention during a work break. The decision had to work in a single glance.
→ Redesigned: message length
💬
Reply signals are informal and varied
Affirmative signals in this context come in multiple regional and informal forms. Rigid "Reply YES" parsing rejected most of them. Flexible natural-language parsing was required.
→ Redesigned: reply parsing
Two operational constraints — not visible without field context
Research also surfaced two hard limits that had to be built into routing logic: natural capacity ceiling (~10–12 jobs/month per supplier before quality declined) and weekly material restocking cycles. Both are industry-specific. Neither appears in a product brief. Routing that ignored them would have produced technically successful assignments that physically couldn't be fulfilled.

Iteration · Build → Learn → Rebuild

Each iteration was narrow by design. One hypothesis, one measurement, one decision.

Rather than rebuilding the full system at once, I sequenced interventions by problem severity and confidence level. This wasn't caution — it was signal preservation. Full rebuilds obscure causation. Narrow changes make it clear what moved the metric.

Strategic Decision · No-Code vs Custom Build

The build decision was a strategy call, not a technical preference.

Before building OMS V2.0, I had a real choice with different risk profiles. I framed it as a strategy question: what does the business need to learn in the next 6 months, and which build path preserves the most optionality while answering that question?

⚖️
The trade-off
No-code (Make.com + Airtable) vs custom development
Chosen · No-code
✓8-week build vs 4+ months
✓~8% monthly cost of equivalent custom-dev maintenance
✓Full internal ownership — changes without developer dependency
✓50× ROI in year one
⚠Ceiling: Make.com complexity hits limits at scale
Rejected · Custom dev
✗4+ month build — delays learning by a quarter
✗3–5× upfront cost with ongoing maintenance dependency
✗Every iteration requires developer time
✗Premature sophistication for unproven routing logic
✓More scalable at high volume — the right call later
The actual bet
Speed of learning over sophistication of output
At this stage, the most valuable thing was learning whether the routing logic worked at all — not building something with headroom for scale we hadn't proven. Fast + owned beats slow + powerful when the hypothesis is still open.
Validated
The accepted risk
No-code has a ceiling — and we'll hit it
At current growth, Make.com scenario complexity becomes a constraint within 12–18 months. The right time to plan migration is before it breaks. This was a deliberate decision to defer, not ignore.
Open risk
What made it work
120 hours of investment before building
I spent 3 weeks learning the stack before writing a single automation — Make.com, Airtable as relational DB, integration patterns. The investment was the decision. It meant every subsequent iteration was fast because there was no dependency.
The 94% principle
Deliberate incompleteness as a design strategy
Targeting 94% auto-routing, not 100%, was a strategic choice. The 6% human override handles cases that no rule can anticipate cleanly — unusual materials, supplier conflicts, complex custom work. Hybrid beats brittle.

Learnings · Obvious and Non-Obvious

What this project taught me that generalises.

Non-obvious
Operational failures are the best product briefs you'll ever get
The Valentine's Day incident gave me more precise problem definition than any discovery workshop would have. Real failures name the exact gap — no format, no acknowledgement, no fallback. I've started treating operational incidents as the most honest user research available.
Non-obvious
Communication design for a supply chain is product design — not ops
The message template — word order, language register, payment placement, reply flexibility — had more measurable impact on system performance than any technical improvement. Most PMs hand that to operations. It needed PM-level attention to information architecture and user behaviour.
Non-obvious
The 8% photo QC non-compliance is a product design failure, not a behaviour problem
8% of suppliers skip the photo step consistently. First instinct: add enforcement. Better question: what makes this step feel like extra work rather than part of the job? The flow asks suppliers to stop, photograph, upload, and wait — that's four interruptions to the craft workflow. The solution is integration, not compliance.
Expected
Narrow iterations preserve causation — wide rebuilds destroy it
I could have rebuilt everything after the first failure. Instead I changed one variable at a time. That discipline made it possible to say with confidence what moved response rate from 68% to 88%. Without it, you have results but no understanding — and no ability to replicate them.
Non-obvious
Ownership is a strategic asset, not a consolation for build constraints
Choosing no-code wasn't about budget — it was about iteration speed. Every improvement to routing logic, every adjustment to the message format, every dashboard change happened same-day because there was no developer queue. That speed compounded. The 4× growth over 27 months was partly a function of how fast the system could adapt to what we learned.
Expected
Progressive rollout is harder to hold to than it sounds under pressure
3 suppliers → 7 → 14 → 30. Each stage revealed failure modes that would have been expensive at full scale. Obvious in principle. In practice, there was constant pressure to move faster — and holding the line on staged rollout was an active, repeated decision, not a plan that ran itself.
